## Cloud vs Self-Hosting

### What determines whether to own or outsource a capability?
<details><summary>Show answer</summary>

Core competency and competitive advantage belong in-house. 
Non-core, routine, or commodity capabilities belong to a vendor — 
the organization that tries to own everything optimizes for the wrong things.

</details>

### How does the own-vs-outsource choice apply to software?
<details><summary>Show answer</summary>

The same own-or-outsource logic splits into two separate questions: 
- **who writes the software** and 
- **who runs it**.


These define a spectrum. At one end sits bespoke software — written and operated in-house, fully owned. 
At the other end sit widely used cloud services or SaaS products — written and run by an outside vendor, 
reached only through a web interface or API. Most real choices fall somewhere between the two ends.

</details>

### What lies between full ownership and a vendor-run service?
<details><summary>Show answer</summary>

Off-the-shelf software — open source or commercial — that **you deploy and run yourself**. 
You don't write it, but you operate it, so you sit between writing your own and renting a finished service.

Where you run it varies:
- **On-premises** — on hardware you control (the term still applies even to a rented rack in someone else's
  datacenter, not just servers physically on your site).
- **IaaS** — on a virtual machine you rent in the cloud; the vendor owns the hardware, but you install and run
  the software on top.

Example: download MySQL and install it on a server you control. The spectrum has finer points too — e.g. taking
open source and running a modified build of it.

</details>

### Beyond who runs software, what deployment question follows?
<details><summary>Show answer</summary>

A separate question is **how** you deploy the services, in the cloud or on premises.

</details>

### What does using a cloud service actually mean?
<details><summary>Show answer</summary>

Using a cloud service instead of running comparable software yourself means you **hand the operation of that
software to the cloud provider**. They run it; you consume it. There are real arguments both for and against
this — covered separately.

</details>

### What governs the self-host vs. cloud choice?
<details><summary>Show answer</summary>

A cloud service hands the *operation* of software to the provider: you trade **control** for **convenience**.
Whether that trade is worth it breaks into separate factors — each is its own card:

Situational fit (does cloud or self-hosting suit you?):
- **Skill** — do you already know how to deploy and run it? See [skill factor](#self-host-vs-cloud--the-skill-factor).
- **Load pattern** — is your machine count steady or spiky? See [load factor](#self-host-vs-cloud--the-load-factor).
- **Tuning** — do you need it tuned to your workload? See [tuning factor](#self-host-vs-cloud--the-tuning-factor).
- **Cost** — what drives the bill at your load? See [cost factor](#self-host-vs-cloud--the-cost-factor).

Fixed cost regardless of fit:
- **Lost control** — six things you give up. See [what you give up](#what-do-you-give-up-with-a-cloud-service).

Trigger to regenerate: *outsourcing operation = less work, less control; four situational factors decide if the
convenience is worth it, plus one fixed loss of control.*

</details>

### Self-host vs. cloud — the skill factor?
<details><summary>Show answer</summary>

If you **already know** how to deploy and operate the system, self-hosting is often cheaper and no harder.
If you **don't**, a cloud service is usually faster than learning it — and hiring or training staff to run
it yourself can get very expensive.

</details>

### Self-host vs. cloud — the load factor?
<details><summary>Show answer</summary>

- **Steady, predictable** load (machine count barely changes) favors self-hosting. 
- **Spiky** load that swings a lot favors cloud, which makes it easy to scale resources up and down as demand changes — 
  so you don't pay for peak capacity that sits idle most of the time.

</details>

### Self-host vs. cloud — the tuning factor?
<details><summary>Show answer</summary>

Run it yourself and you can **configure and tune** it for your own workload. 
A provider likely won't make those customizations for you — but their edge is **operational experience** 
gained from running the service for many customers, which can mean a better baseline.

</details>

### Self-host vs. cloud — the cost factor?
<details><summary>Show answer</summary>

At **steady, predictable** load, owning your own machines is often cheaper. 


Cloud wins when load **varies** — provisioning for peak means resources sit idle most of the time, 
so paying only for what you use is more cost-effective. Either way, cloud doesn't remove the ops team; 
it frees them from basic sysadmin to focus on higher-level concerns.

</details>

### What do you give up with a cloud service?
<details><summary>Show answer</summary>

One root loss — **you don't control the software**:

- **Missing feature** — you can't add it yourself; you can only ask the vendor and hope.
- **Outage** — you can't dig in; you wait for them to recover.
- **Hard to debug** — no access to OS metrics, internals, or server logs, so bugs and slowdowns are opaque.
- **Lock-in** — if they shut down, raise prices, or change the product, you can't keep the old version; you're
  forced to migrate. Worse when no standard API exists to switch against.
- **Geopolitics** — a provider in another country can be cut off by sanctions if relations sour.
- **Data trust** — you must trust them with your data, which complicates privacy and compliance.

Despite this, cloud and hybrid setups keep growing — but won't replace everything: 
legacy systems and specialist needs (e.g. very latency-sensitive ones) still require in-house.

</details>

### What architecture is built around cloud services?
<details><summary>Show answer</summary>

**Cloud native** — an architecture designed from the start to take advantage of cloud services, rather than
software merely moved to the cloud unchanged. The cloud changed two things: the **economic model** (subscribe
to a service instead of buying hardware and licensing software) and, on a technical level, **how data systems
are built** — cloud-native is the name for that second shift.

</details>

### Examples of self-hosted vs. cloud-native databases?
<details><summary>Show answer</summary>

Two splits: **self-hosted vs. cloud-native** × **operational (OLTP) vs. analytical (OLAP)**.

| Category             | Self-hosted                 | Cloud-native                                              |
|----------------------|-----------------------------|-----------------------------------------------------------|
| **Operational/OLTP** | MySQL, PostgreSQL, MongoDB  | AWS Aurora, Azure SQL DB Hyperscale, Google Cloud Spanner |
| **Analytical/OLAP**  | Teradata, ClickHouse, Spark | Snowflake, Google BigQuery, Azure Synapse Analytics       |

</details>

### What does self-hosted software need to run?
<details><summary>Show answer</summary>

Mostly **generic computing resources**: CPU, RAM, a filesystem to store data as files, and an IP network
(TCP/IP) to communicate. It runs on a conventional OS like Linux or Windows. A few systems need special
hardware — GPUs for ML, or RDMA network interfaces — but those are the exception. Because the needs are so
ordinary, this software ports to the cloud with no change → which is what IaaS offers.

</details>

### What does IaaS give you?
<details><summary>Show answer</summary>

A virtual machine that behaves like a **normal computer you rent instead of own** — an allocation of CPU,
memory, disk, and network bandwidth. You can run any software on it, but you administer it yourself (OS,
patching, the software, all of it). Versus a physical machine, instances are provisioned far faster and come
in more sizes — but otherwise it's the same deal. So IaaS is the cloud *imitating* a traditional computer →
cloud-native breaks from that.

</details>

### What makes cloud-native different from a cloud VM?
<details><summary>Show answer</summary>

A cloud VM (IaaS) only gives you OS-managed resources — you still build on a raw filesystem and raw machines.
**Cloud-native instead builds on higher-level cloud services.** Example: object storage like Amazon S3, Azure
Blob Storage, or Cloudflare R2 replaces "the filesystem." The API is more limited (basic reads and writes),
but the service hides the physical machines underneath → which is where the real gain comes from.

</details>

### What do you gain from a higher-level service?
<details><summary>Show answer</summary>

Using S3 as the example: it **hides the machines** and handles what you'd otherwise manage yourself. It spreads
data across many machines automatically, so you never worry about one machine running out of disk; and if some
machines or disks fail entirely, no data is lost. You trade a richer filesystem API for not having to manage
capacity or failure. And because these services are reusable building blocks, they stack.

</details>

### How do cloud services stack?
<details><summary>Show answer</summary>

Cloud-native is **layered** — each service builds on the one below. Object storage (S3) sits near the bottom.
Snowflake, a cloud analytical database, builds on S3 for its storage. Other services, in turn, build on
Snowflake. So you get a stack: raw storage → database → tools on top of the database → and so on. The open
question that follows: which layer should *you* build on?

</details>

### How do you choose which layer to build on?
<details><summary>Show answer</summary>

Trigger: *self-hosted needs generic resources → IaaS rents them as a VM → cloud-native builds on services
instead → which gives capacity/failure handling for free → services stack on services → so you choose a layer
by the control-vs-hassle trade-off.*

The trade-off rule: **higher-level abstractions are more specialized.** If your need matches what a high-level
service was built for, use it — far less hassle than assembling it yourself. If nothing fits your need, drop
down and build it from lower-level components — that's then your only option.

The whole topic in one frame: *IaaS treats the cloud as a rented computer; cloud-native treats the cloud as a
stack of services you build on top of. The deeper you build from raw components, the more control and effort;
the higher you build, the less hassle but the more you're locked to one use case.*

</details>

### How is storage durability handled traditionally?
<details><summary>Show answer</summary>

Disk is treated as **durable** — once written, assumed not lost. To survive a single disk failing, **RAID**
keeps copies across several disks on the **same machine**. RAID can be done in hardware or by the OS, and it's
transparent to the application using the filesystem. Baseline assumption: storage and compute sit on one
machine, and the local disk is trusted. The cloud breaks that → next.

</details>

### Why cant a cloud VM treat local disk as durable?
<details><summary>Show answer</summary>

A cloud instance's local disk is **ephemeral**, so cloud-native systems treat it as a cache, not long-term
storage. The disk becomes inaccessible if the instance fails, or if the instance is swapped for a bigger or
smaller one — that lands on a different physical machine, leaving the old disk behind. So durable storage has
to live somewhere other than local disk → first attempt: virtual disks.

</details>

### What does a virtual disk fix, and what does it cost?
<details><summary>Show answer</summary>

Virtual disks (Amazon EBS, Azure managed disks, GCP persistent disks) are block storage you can **detach from
one instance and attach to another**. It's not a physical disk — it's a separate set of machines emulating a
block device (blocks typically 4 KiB). This lets traditional disk-based software run in the cloud, but the
emulation adds overhead, and **every I/O is a network call**, so the app is very sensitive to network glitches.
A patch for old software, not a native design → cloud-native goes further.

</details>

### What do cloud-native systems use for storage instead?
<details><summary>Show answer</summary>

They avoid virtual disks and build on **dedicated storage services tuned to the workload**. Object storage like
S3 is built for long-term storage of fairly large files (hundreds of KB to several GB). But database
rows/values are much smaller than that — so cloud databases keep small values in a **separate service** and
pack many values into **large blocks stored in the object store**. Using a storage service distinct from the
compute machine forces a deeper structural change → next.

</details>

### What structural shift does this produce?
<details><summary>Show answer</summary>

Storage and compute, traditionally on the **same machine** (disk + CPU/RAM together), become **separated —
"disaggregated."** Example: S3 only stores files; to analyze that data you run compute *outside* S3. That move
has two consequences, which branch from here:
- it forces data over the network at analysis time — see [analysis cost](#what-does-separation-cost-at-analysis-time);
- it lets one shared service serve many customers — see [multitenancy](#what-does-sharing-the-separated-hardware-introduce).

</details>

### What does separation cost at analysis time?
<details><summary>Show answer</summary>

Because storage and compute no longer share a machine, the data must **travel over the network** to wherever the
compute runs. S3 holds the files; the analysis code runs elsewhere and pulls the data across the network to work
on it. (This is the cost side of disaggregation — convenience of independent storage, paid for in network
transfer.)

</details>

### What does sharing the separated hardware introduce?
<details><summary>Show answer</summary>

**Multitenancy** — instead of one machine per customer, several customers' data and computation run on the
**same shared hardware** behind the same service. Upside: better hardware use, easier scaling, easier management
for the provider. Cost: it takes careful engineering so one customer's activity can't hurt another's
**performance or security** (the isolation problem).

</details>

### Who traditionally ran data infrastructure?
<details><summary>Show answer</summary>

**Database administrators (DBAs)** and **system administrators (sysadmins)**. More recently many organizations
merged software development and operations into one team with shared responsibility for backend services and
data infrastructure — the **DevOps** philosophy. **Site reliability engineers (SREs)** are Google's version of
that idea. Sets up *what* this role is actually for → next.

</details>

### What is the job of operations?
<details><summary>Show answer</summary>

Two goals: deliver services **reliably** to users (configure infrastructure, deploy applications) and keep a
**stable production environment** (monitor and diagnose anything threatening reliability). For self-hosted
systems this traditionally meant heavy **per-machine** work: capacity planning (watch disk space, add disks
before running out), provisioning new machines, moving services between machines, installing OS patches. The
cloud changes what this work looks like → next.

</details>

### How does a cloud API change operations work?
<details><summary>Show answer</summary>

A cloud service exposes an **API that hides the individual machines** behind it. Example: cloud storage
replaces fixed-size disks with **metered billing** — store data without planning capacity ahead, pay for what
you use. Many cloud services also stay **highly available even when individual machines fail**. So the focus
shifts **from individual machines to services** — same reliability goal, but different tools and processes →
which is what DevOps/SRE codifies.

</details>

### What does DevOps/SRE emphasize?
<details><summary>Show answer</summary>

The reliability goal is unchanged; the practices evolved. DevOps/SRE leans on:
- **automation** — repeatable processes over manual one-off jobs;
- **ephemeral** VMs and services rather than long-running servers;
- **frequent** application updates;
- **learning from incidents**;
- **preserving system knowledge** in the org even as people come and go.

This philosophy, plus cloud, splits the operations role itself → next.

</details>

### How did cloud split the operations role in two?
<details><summary>Show answer</summary>

A **bifurcation** of roles. Operations teams at **infrastructure companies** specialize in delivering a
reliable service to many customers. The **customers** of those services aim to spend as little time and effort
on infrastructure as possible. So "operations" now means two different jobs depending on which side you're on.
The customer side still carries real work, and some of it can't be handed off at all — two branches:
- what the customer still does — see [customer operations](#what-does-cloud-operations-still-demand-of-the-customer);
- what stays in-house regardless — see [never outsourced](#what-can-never-be-outsourced-to-the-cloud).

</details>

### What does cloud operations still demand of the customer?
<details><summary>Show answer</summary>

Different work, not less. Customers still must **choose** the right service for a task, **integrate** services
with each other, and **migrate** between services. Metered billing removes traditional capacity planning, but
you must still track what you use and why — so **capacity planning becomes financial planning, and performance
optimization becomes cost optimization**. Services also have **quotas/limits** (e.g. max concurrent processes)
you must know and plan around. And **integration** is a growing challenge — many vendors, ever more services,
and no standards, so it often takes significant manual effort.

</details>

### What can never be outsourced to the cloud?
<details><summary>Show answer</summary>

Some operations stay yours no matter what: keeping your **application and its libraries secure**, managing the
**interactions between your own services**, **monitoring the load** on your services, and **tracking down
causes** of problems like performance degradation or outages. The cloud changes the role of operations — it
does not remove the need for it.

</details>
