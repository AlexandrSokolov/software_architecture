## Operational vs. Analytical Systems

### One spine on the topic
<details><summary>Show answer</summary>

- Data systems split into operational (OLTP) and analytical (OLAP) — by access pattern, not by who uses them.
- Analytical data gets its own home (warehouse → lake), filled by ETL / pipelines.
- Underneath it all, one orientation: some data is the source of truth, the rest is derived from it.

</details>

### What defines a data-intensive application?
<details><summary>Show answer</summary>

An application is **data-intensive** when data management is one of the primary challenges in developing it.

</details>

### What is the counterpart to a data-intensive system, and how do its challenges differ?
<details><summary>Show answer</summary>

The counterpart is the **compute-intensive system**.

- **Compute-intensive** — the challenge is parallelizing a very large computation.
- **Data-intensive** — the concerns are storing and processing large data volumes, managing changes to
  data, ensuring consistency in the face of failures and concurrency, and keeping services highly available.

</details>

### What general-purpose functions surface again and again when building data-intensive applications?
<details><summary>Show answer</summary>

Applications are typically assembled from standard building blocks:

- **Databases** — store data so it can be found again later.
- **Caches** — remember the result of an expensive operation to speed up reads.
- **Search indexes** — allow data to be searched by keyword or filtered in various ways.
- **Stream processing** — handle events and data changes as soon as they occur.
- **Batch processing** — periodically crunch a large amount of accumulated data.

</details>

### Into what fundamental categories are data-intensive systems commonly divided?
<details><summary>Show answer</summary>

The split runs between two system types:

- **Operational systems** — the backend services and data infrastructure where data is created 
  (e.g. by serving external users). Application code both reads and modifies the data in its databases, 
  based on user actions. Built and maintained by **backend engineers**, who write the application code
  that serves users and keep the live service correct, fast, and available.
- **Analytical systems** — serve business analysts and data scientists. They hold a read-only copy of the
  data from operational systems, optimized for the kinds of data processing that analytics requires.

</details>

### Business analysts vs. data scientists?
<details><summary>Show answer</summary>

**Different goals:**

- **Business analysts** — generate reports on the organization's activities to help management make better
  decisions (business intelligence, BI). They answer *known questions* — e.g. "what were last quarter's sales
  by region?" The metric is predefined; the work is computing and presenting it.
- **Data scientists** — look for novel insights or build user-facing product features enabled by data analysis
  and ML/AI (e.g. "people who bought X also bought Y" recommendations, risk scoring, spam filtering, search
  ranking). They hunt for things nobody thought to ask — hidden patterns, correlations, predictive signals
  buried in the data. *Novel* = the insight didn't exist as a question beforehand; it's discovered.

**Shared practices:**

- Both perform **analytics** — they look at data generated by users and backend services.
- Both generally **do not modify** that data (beyond fixing mistakes), though they may create derived datasets.

</details>

### What engineering roles emerged to support operational and analytical systems?
<details><summary>Show answer</summary>

- **Data engineers** — integrate the operational and analytical systems and take responsibility for the
  organization's data infrastructure more widely.
- **Analytics engineers** — model and transform data to make it more useful for the business analysts and
  data scientists in the organization.

</details>

### Who builds, bridges, and consumes data across operational and analytical systems, and where do the roles overlap?
<details><summary>Show answer</summary>

Trace one record — say, a purchase — through the pipeline. Each role owns a different stage.

| Role                   | System                            | What they do                                                                                          | Data is…                          |
|------------------------|-----------------------------------|-------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------|-----------------------------------|
| **Backend engineer**   | Operational                       | Writes the app code that serves users; reads + modifies the DB on user actions                        | A *means* to run the live product |
| **Data engineer**      | Bridge (operational ↔ analytical) | Integrates the two sides; moves data reliably into the analytical world; owns the data infrastructure | The *deliverable* itself          |
| **Analytics engineer** | Analytical                        | Models and transforms raw data into clean, useful tables for downstream consumers                     | Raw input to be *shaped*          |
| **Business analyst**   | Analytical                        | Answers *known questions* (e.g. "last quarter's sales by region") for BI / management decisions       | Something to *report on*          |
| **Data scientist**     | Analytical                        | Hunts for *novel insights* and builds data/ML-powered product features                                | Something to *discover from*      |

**The journey of one purchase:**

1. **Backend engineer** — records the purchase into the operational DB. *(data created)*
2. **Data engineer** — pipes that purchase, with millions of others, into the warehouse, reliably and on
   schedule, without breaking the live DB. *(data moved)*
3. **Analytics engineer** — transforms raw purchases into modeled tables like "daily revenue by region."
   *(data shaped)*
4. **Business analyst / data scientist** — query those tables for reports or insights. *(data consumed)*

**Nuances and overlaps:**

- **Backend vs. data engineer** — both work with databases and infrastructure, but the backend engineer
  sits *inside* the live product (optimizing latency and availability for external users), while the data
  engineer is *plumbing between systems* (optimizing throughput and reliability for internal consumers).
  At small companies, one person often plays both.
- **Business analyst vs. data scientist** — both read analytical data and generally don't modify it, but
  the analyst answers predefined questions whereas the scientist discovers questions nobody had asked.
- **Analytics engineer vs. data engineer** — the data engineer gets data *there* (movement, infrastructure);
  the analytics engineer makes it *useful* (modeling, transformation). The line between them is often blurry.

</details>

### Beyond who uses the data, what two contrasting ways of accessing it emerge in business data processing?
<details><summary>Show answer</summary>

Independent of *who* runs the workload, business data processing divides by **access pattern**:

- **OLTP** (Online Transaction Processing) — many small, fast reads and writes driven by user actions;
  the live, operational workload.
- **OLAP** (Online Analytical Processing) — fewer, larger queries that scan and aggregate big volumes of
  data for analysis.

The distinction is about the *shape of the workload*, not the job title of the person issuing it.

**A third category** sits between them: **product / real-time analytics** (Pinot, Druid, ClickHouse) —
OLAP-shaped queries (aggregate over many records) but embedded in user-facing products, ingesting data
in real time and optimized for low-latency responses rather than throughput.

</details>

### Which read and write properties distinguish OLTP from OLAP?
<details><summary>Show answer</summary>

| Property               | OLTP (operational)                              | OLAP (analytical)                        |
|------------------------|-------------------------------------------------|------------------------------------------|
| **Main read pattern**  | Point queries — fetch individual records by key | Aggregate over a large number of records |
| **Main write pattern** | Create, update, delete individual records       | Bulk import (ETL) or event stream        |

</details>

### Which query properties distinguish OLTP from OLAP?
<details><summary>Show answer</summary>

| Property            | OLTP (operational)                   | OLAP (analytical)                         |
|---------------------|--------------------------------------|-------------------------------------------|
| **Type of queries** | Fixed, predefined by the application | Arbitrary, ad-hoc exploration by analysts |
| **Query volume**    | Lots of small queries                | Few queries, each one complex             |


- **OLTP** — users can't write custom queries; they'd risk reading/modifying unauthorized data or running
  expensive queries that hurt performance for others. So queries are fixed and baked into the application
  code, with one-off custom queries only for maintenance or troubleshooting.
- **OLAP** — users are free to write arbitrary SQL by hand, or generate it via a visualization/dashboard
  tool (Tableau, Looker, Power BI).

</details>

### Which data properties distinguish OLTP from OLAP?
<details><summary>Show answer</summary>

| Property            | OLTP (operational)                               | OLAP (analytical)                         |
|---------------------|--------------------------------------------------|-------------------------------------------|
| **Data represents** | Latest state of the data (current point in time) | History of events that happened over time |
| **Dataset size**    | Gigabytes to terabytes                           | Terabytes to petabytes                    |

</details>

### What distinguishes traditional OLAP from real-time / product analytics?
<details><summary>Show answer</summary>

Both run analytical queries (aggregate over many records), 
but they serve different consumers and optimize for opposite goals.

| Property             | Traditional OLAP                           | Real-time / product analytics            |
|----------------------|--------------------------------------------|------------------------------------------|
| **Consumer**         | Internal analysts                          | End users (in-product)                   |
| **Ingest**           | Batches (e.g. overnight ETL)               | Real time / streaming                    |
| **Optimized for**    | Throughput — crunch huge volumes per query | Low latency — instant response per query |
| **Tolerates delay?** | Yes — a human waits for a report           | No — it's part of the product UX         |
| **Examples**         | Data warehouses                            | Pinot, Druid, ClickHouse                 |

The names capture it: **product analytics** = analytics embedded in a product; **real-time analytics** =
analytics on freshly-ingested data with instant responses.

</details>

### What shift separated analytical querying from the transactional database that originally hosted it?
<details><summary>Show answer</summary>

At first, the **same database** served both transaction processing and analytical queries — 
SQL was flexible enough to handle both reasonably well.

In the **late 1980s and early 1990s**, a trend arose for companies to stop running analytics on their OLTP
systems and instead move analytical queries onto a **separate database system** — the **data warehouse**.

</details>

### What problem does a data warehouse solve, and how is it built?
<details><summary>Show answer</summary>

**The problem.** A large enterprise runs many OLTP systems (website, point-of-sale, inventory, routing, suppliers, HR, …), 
each complex and operated independently. Querying them directly for analytics is undesirable because:

- Data of interest is spread across multiple systems, hard to combine in one query (**data silos**).
- OLTP-friendly schemas are poorly suited for analytics.
- Expensive analytical queries would hurt OLTP performance for other users.
- OLTP systems may sit in restricted networks (security/compliance).


**The solution.** A **data warehouse** is a separate database holding a **read-only copy** of data from all
the company's OLTP systems. Analysts can query it freely without affecting operations, and it stores data
in a layout optimized for analytics.


**How it's built (ETL).** Data is **extracted** from OLTP databases (periodic dump or continuous stream),
**transformed** into an analysis-friendly schema and cleaned up, then **loaded** into the warehouse.

</details>

### What does ETL stand for, and what happens at each step?
<details><summary>Show answer</summary>

**ETL** = **Extract–Transform–Load** — the process of getting data into a data warehouse.

- **Extract** — pull data from OLTP databases (via a periodic dump or a continuous stream of updates).
- **Transform** — reshape it into an analysis-friendly schema and clean it up.
- **Load** — write it into the data warehouse.

</details>

### What's different about ETL when the source isn't a database you own?
<details><summary>Show answer</summary>

Sometimes the source is an external SaaS product — CRM, email marketing, credit card processing — rather
than your own database.

**What changes:** you have no direct database access. The data lives on the vendor's servers, reachable
only through their **API**. So *extract* stops being a table read and becomes "repeatedly call the vendor's
API and collect what it returns."

**Why pull it into your own warehouse anyway:** the SaaS API alone is limited — it can't:
- join data across multiple vendors, 
- run heavy historical analysis, or 
- combine with your internal data. 

Landing it in your warehouse turns it into tables you can query and join freely.

**Why specialist tools exist:** every vendor's API differs (auth, formats, pagination, rate limits) and
changes over time, so building a connector per product is painful plumbing. Services like **Fivetran**,
**Singer**, and **Airbyte** provide pre-built, maintained connectors — essentially the *extract* step as a
managed service for SaaS APIs.

</details>

### As workloads grow, what happens to systems that aren't specialized for one task?
<details><summary>Show answer</summary>

**General-purpose system** — a system not specialized for any one workload (e.g. a database serving both
transactions and analytics). It handles small data volumes comfortably, but as scale grows it gives way to
**specialized systems**, each optimized for a particular workload. This is a wider trend: the greater the
scale, the more specialized systems tend to become.

**HTAP** (Hybrid Transactional/Analytical Processing) is one specific general-purpose design — a system
that deliberately aims to support both OLTP and analytics together, avoiding ETL between them. It sits as a
named member under the broader general-purpose umbrella.

**Why HTAP doesn't simply replace the data warehouse:**

- **Often not truly unified** — many HTAP systems are internally an OLTP system coupled with a separate
  analytical one behind a common interface, so the OLTP/OLAP distinction still matters.
- **Doesn't replace the warehouse** — each operational system tends to have its own database (potentially
  hundreds), while an enterprise wants a single warehouse to join across them all.
- **Fits a narrow case** — useful when one application needs both large analytical scans and low-latency
  reads/updates of individual records (e.g. fraud detection).

**The mental hook:** general-purpose = the broad class that loses at scale; HTAP = one specific OLTP+OLAP
design you'd reach for in a narrow case, not a general replacement for the warehouse.

</details>

### What is the warehouse's usual data model, what limitation does it have, and what solves it?
<details><summary>Show answer</summary>

**The model.** A data warehouse usually uses a **relational data model** queried through SQL. 
This works well for the queries business analysts make.

**The limitation.** It's less suited to what data scientists do:

- **Feature engineering** — transforming table rows/columns into a vector or matrix of numerical values
  (features) to train an ML model, in a way that maximizes performance. This often needs custom code that's
  hard to express in SQL.
- **NLP / computer vision** — extracting structured information from text (e.g. sentiment or topics from
  product reviews) or from photos.

As a result, many data scientists prefer not to work in a relational warehouse, reaching instead for Python
libraries (Pandas, scikit-learn), statistical languages (R), and distributed frameworks (Spark).

**The solution.** A **data lake** — a centralized repository holding a copy of any data that might be useful
for analysis, obtained from operational systems via ETL.

</details>

### What is a data lake, and how does it differ from a data warehouse?
<details><summary>Show answer</summary>

A **data lake** is a centralized repository holding a copy of any data that might be useful for analysis,
obtained from operational systems via ETL.

**Difference from a warehouse:** a data lake simply contains **files**, imposing no particular file format,
data model, or schema. Those files might be database records encoded as Avro or Parquet, but equally text,
images, videos, sensor readings, sparse matrices, feature vectors, genome sequences, or anything else. It's
both more **flexible** and often **cheaper** than relational storage, since it can use commoditized file
storage such as object stores.

**Raw data and the sushi principle:** ETL has generalized into **data pipelines**, and the lake is often an
intermediate stop on the path from operational systems to the warehouse. The lake holds data in its **raw**
form — untransformed into any warehouse schema — so each consumer can transform it into the shape that best
suits their needs. This is sometimes called the **sushi principle**: "raw data is better."

</details>

### How can analytical processing differ in its responsiveness to incoming data?
<details><summary>Show answer</summary>

Analytical data increasingly arrives not only as files and relational tables, but as **streams of events**.

- **File-based** — rerun the analysis periodically (e.g. daily) to respond to changes in the data.
- **Stream processing** — respond to events much faster, on the order of seconds.

Depending on the application's time-sensitivity, the stream approach can be valuable — for example, to
identify and block potentially fraudulent or abusive activity in near real time.

</details>

### Systems of Record vs Derived Data
<details><summary>Show answer</summary>

**System of record** (source of truth) — holds the authoritative, canonical version of data. New data is
written here first, each fact represented exactly once (typically normalized). If another system disagrees
with it, the system of record is by definition correct.

**Derived data system** — the result of taking data from another system and transforming or processing it.
If lost, it can be re-created from the original source. Examples: caches, denormalized values, indexes,
materialized views, transformed representations, and trained ML models.

**Why derived data exists despite being redundant:** it duplicates existing information, but is often
essential for read performance — and you can derive several datasets from one source to view the data from
different angles.

**Where each appears:**

- **Analytical systems** are usually derived (they consume data created elsewhere).
- **Operational services** mix both: systems of record are the primary databases written to first; derived
  systems are the indexes and caches that speed up reads the system of record can't answer efficiently.

**The distinction is about usage, not the tool.** Most databases are neither inherently — a database is just
a tool; whether data is "of record" or "derived" depends on how you use it in your application.

**Why it matters:** being clear about which data is derived from which brings clarity to an otherwise
confusing architecture. It also surfaces a real problem — when one system's data is derived from another,
you need a process to update the derived data when the source changes (addressed later via data pipelines).

</details>
