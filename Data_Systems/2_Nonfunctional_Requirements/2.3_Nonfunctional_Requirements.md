## Nonfunctional requirements - Scalability

### What is scalability?
<details><summary>Show answer</summary>

A system's ability to handle **more load** without breaking down or slowing to a crawl — by adding resources as
demand grows.


It's not a yes/no property: you ask:
- "scalable along which axis?" (more users, more data, more requests) and
- "by adding what?" (bigger machines, or more of them).

</details>

### Investing in scalability — what to consider?
<details><summary>Show answer</summary>

Match the investment to the product's stage.

Early, with few users, the priority is keeping the system **simple and flexible** so you can change features fast
as you learn what customers need. Building for hypothetical future scale is **premature optimization**:

- best case, the effort is wasted;
- worst case, it locks you into an inflexible design that's harder to evolve.

</details>

### How do you reason about scalability?
<details><summary>Show answer</summary>

1. [**Measure current load**](#how-do-you-describe-a-systems-load) first —
   you can't answer "what if load doubles?" without knowing today's load.
2. [**See what growth does**](#what-happens-when-load-grows) — increase load and watch what breaks or what it costs.
3. [**Judge the cost of growth**](#what-is-linear-scalability) — does handling 2× load need 2× resources, or more?

Goal throughout: **stay within the SLA while keeping cost as low as possible**.

</details>

### How do you describe a system's load?
<details><summary>Show answer</summary>

Pick the numbers that match how your system is actually used.

- Usually **throughput** — requests/sec, GB of new data/day, checkouts/hour.
- Sometimes the **peak** of a varying quantity — e.g. simultaneous online users.
- Often the **shape** of the load matters too — read/write ratio, cache hit rate, items per user.

And decide whether the **average case** drives you, or a few **extreme cases** are the real bottleneck.
It depends on the application.

</details>

### What happens when load grows?
<details><summary>Show answer</summary>

Two ways to look at the same growth:

- **Fixed resources** — keep CPU, memory, bandwidth the same, add load: how much does performance degrade?
- **Fixed performance** — keep performance the same, add load: how much extra resource must you add?

Both are asking how the system holds up as demand rises; you pick the one that fits the question at hand.

</details>

### What is linear scalability?
<details><summary>Show answer</summary>

**Linear scalability** — double the resources, handle double the load, same performance. This is the good case.

Rarely you do better than linear (economies of scale, better peak spreading). Far more often cost grows **faster
than linear** — e.g. with lots of data, one write can involve more work than the same write on a small dataset.

</details>

### What are the principles for scalability?
<details><summary>Show answer</summary>

Three guiding principles:

- [**No magic sauce**](#universal-scalable-architecture) — scalable architecture is application-specific,
  and each 10× load needs a rethink.
- [**Decompose**](#key-principle-for-building-scalable-systems) — split into parts that work independently; the hard
  part is where to draw the line.
- [**Stay simple**](#what-guards-against-over-engineering-scale) — don't add complexity you don't need; good designs
  are a pragmatic mix.

</details>

### Universal scalable architecture
<details><summary>Show answer</summary>

There's no "magic scaling sauce." A scalable architecture is always specific to the application. Two systems
with the same total throughput can look completely different: 100,000 tiny requests/sec vs 3 huge requests/min both
move 100 MB/s, but need opposite designs.

And an architecture built for one load level usually won't survive 10× that load. On a fast-growing service, expect
to rethink the architecture at **every order-of-magnitude** increase — so don't plan more than one order of
magnitude ahead.

</details>

### Key principle for building scalable systems?
<details><summary>Show answer</summary>

Break the system into smaller parts that can work **largely independently** of one another.
This is the idea under microservices, sharding, stream processing, and shared-nothing architectures.


The hard part isn't the splitting — it's knowing **where to draw the line**: what belongs together vs what belongs
apart.

</details>

### What guards against over-engineering scale?
<details><summary>Show answer</summary>

Don't make things more complex than they need to be.

- If a single-machine database does the job, prefer it over a distributed setup.
- Autoscaling is nice, but with predictable load a manually scaled system has fewer surprises.
- 5 services are simpler to run than 50.

Good architectures are a pragmatic mix, not the maximally clever option.

</details>
