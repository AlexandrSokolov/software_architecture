## Nonfunctional requirements - Reliability

### Fault vs failure?
<details><summary>Show answer</summary>

Same idea at two levels of scope: what stopped working, the part or the whole.

- Fault: one part stops working correctly — a disk dies, a machine crashes, a dependency has an outage.
- Failure (outage): the whole system stops giving the user the required service — it breaks its SLO.

The relationship: one event can be either, depending on where you stand. A dead disk is a failure of that disk,
but only a fault to the larger system around it. If that system has a copy elsewhere, it tolerates the fault and
never reaches failure. So faults are local and survivable; a failure is a fault that the system failed to absorb.

</details>

### What is Fault Tolerance?
<details><summary>Show answer</summary>

A system is **fault-tolerant** if it keeps serving users despite certain faults.

Example:
A file saved on two disks. One disk dies. The system reads the copy from the other disk and keeps working —
the user notices nothing. That's fault tolerance: one part failed, the whole kept serving.

</details>

### Where does one fault take down everything?
<details><summary>Show answer</summary>

A **single point of failure (SPOF)** — a part the system cannot operate without.
There's no backup, so a fault there escalates straight into a whole-system failure.

Example: one load balancer in front of ten healthy app servers. The servers are redundant; the load balancer isn't.
It dies, and all ten become unreachable — one fault, total outage (failure). That single balancer is the SPOF.

</details>

### How do you know fault tolerance actually works?
<details><summary>Show answer</summary>

Break things on purpose. **Fault injection** deliberately triggers faults — e.g. randomly killing processes — so
the fault-tolerance machinery runs constantly instead of sitting untested until a real fault hits. Error-handling
paths that never run are the ones that fail when needed.

**Chaos engineering** is the discipline built around this.

</details>

### What are common hardware faults?
<details><summary>Show answer</summary>

Individual physical components failing:

- **Hard drives** — mechanical failure, the classic case in any large disk fleet.
- **SSDs** — wear plus occasional uncorrectable bit errors.
- **RAID controllers, power supplies, memory modules** — fail less often than disks but still fail.
- **CPUs** — a small fraction have cores that occasionally compute the wrong result, sometimes silently.
- **RAM** — bits corrupt from cosmic rays or defects; even ECC memory hits uncorrectable errors.

Rare on a small system, but at scale they happen often enough to be normal operation.

</details>

### Tolerating hardware faults — cloud vs single node?
<details><summary>Show answer</summary>

Two different levels of defense.

- **Single-node systems** add **hardware redundancy** inside one machine: RAID across disks, dual power supplies,
  hot-swappable CPUs, backup generators. This keeps one machine running for years — but it works best when faults
  are independent, and in practice failures are often correlated (a whole rack or datacenter can go down at once).
- **Cloud systems** care less about any single machine. They tolerate faulty nodes **in software**, spreading
  across machines so the service survives even a full datacenter outage. **Availability zones** mark which
  resources are physically co-located, so you can place redundancy in separate zones.


Tolerating machine loss also helps operations. A single-server system needs planned downtime to reboot for
patches; a multi-node system does a **rolling upgrade** — restart one node at a time, no service interruption.

</details>

### Hardware faults vs software faults?
<details><summary>Show answer</summary>

The key difference is **correlation**, and it drives how much damage each does.

- **Hardware faults** — mostly **independent**. One disk failing says nothing about the next; other components
  usually keep working, at least for a while. Redundancy handles them well.
- **Software faults** — often **highly correlated**. Many nodes run the same code, so they share the same bug —
  it can hit them all at once. Harder to anticipate, and they cause far more system failures than hardware faults.

Redundant hardware doesn't help: identical copies just fail the same way together.

</details>

### Who is to blame for a preventable mistake?
<details><summary>Show answer</summary>

Usually the **organization's priorities**, not the person. Faced with more features vs. more testing, teams
reasonably pick features — so when the predictable mistake happens, blaming the individual misses the real cause.

</details>

### How should you respond to an incident?
<details><summary>Show answer</summary>

Separate two questions and ask them in different rooms.

- **Incident investigation → blameless.** People share full details without fear of punishment, so others can
  learn to prevent it. This may surface a need to shift priorities, fund neglected areas, or fix incentives. Be
  suspicious of simplistic answers — neither "Bob should've been careful" nor "rewrite it in Haskell" helps. Learn
  how the **sociotechnical system** really works from the people in it, and improve it from that.
- **Performance management → separate track.** Blameless is about the incident, not a claim that competence never
  matters. Whether a person is skilled and trying is a real question — handled through review, pairing, and honest
  feedback, and if it points at hiring, at the interview and review process. Just don't run it *during* the
  postmortem: blame at the moment of failure buys silence and kills the learning.

</details>

### What metrics exist to measure reliability?
<details><summary>Show answer</summary>

Two independent axes, combined into one overall number.

1. **How often it breaks** → [MTBF](#how-often-does-it-break) — the average gap between outages.
2. **How fast it recovers** → [MTTR](#how-fast-does-it-recover) — the average time to restore service.
3. **How available it is overall** → [availability](#how-available-is-the-system-overall) — one number combining both.

Trigger: reliability isn't one number — it's *breaks-how-often* and *recovers-how-fast*, and those are separate.
A system can score well on one and badly on the other; availability is what you get when you fold them together.

</details>

### How often does it break?
<details><summary>Show answer</summary>

**MTBF (Mean Time Between Failures)** — the average time a system runs before it fails again.
Bigger MTBF = breaks less often.

One split, by whether the broken unit gets fixed or thrown away:

- **MTBF** — for things you **repair** and keep running (a server: fails, you fix it, it runs, it fails again — so
  you can measure the gap *between* failures).
- **MTTF (Mean Time To Failure)** — for things you **replace, not repair** (a disk: it dies once, that unit has no
  "next" failure — so you measure time *to* the one failure, averaged over many units).

Same question either way — how long until it breaks — just measured differently for repairable vs throwaway parts.

</details>

### How fast does it recover?
<details><summary>Show answer</summary>

**MTTR (Mean Time To Recovery)** — the average time to restore service after an outage starts.
Smaller MTTR = shorter outages.

It covers the whole recovery span, not just the fix: detect the outage → diagnose → repair → verify service is back.
Faster detection and faster failover cut MTTR just as much as a faster fix does.

Why it matters as its own axis: it's the lever you control *after* something breaks. You can't stop every fault,
but you can shorten how long each one hurts — and [availability](#how-available-is-the-system-overall) rewards that directly.

</details>

### How available is the system overall?
<details><summary>Show answer</summary>

**Availability** — the fraction of time the system is serving correctly. The one number that folds both axes together.

Split one outage cycle into two stretches:

- **up-stretch** — time running between failures → measured by **MTBF** (bigger = better)
- **down-stretch** — time broken during a failure → measured by **MTTR** (smaller = better)

Availability = up-time out of total time:

    Availability = MTBF / (MTBF + MTTR)

The insight most people miss: **you raise availability by shrinking MTTR, not only by growing MTBF.**
A system that breaks often but recovers in seconds can beat one that breaks rarely but stays down for hours.
Recovery speed is a design lever, not just failure prevention.

</details>

### What does "four nines" mean?
<details><summary>Show answer</summary>

Availability written as a percent of nines — each extra nine cuts allowed downtime by 10×.

| Availability | Name        | Downtime per year |
|--------------|-------------|-------------------|
| 99%          | two nines   | ~3.65 days        |
| 99.9%        | three nines | ~8.8 hours        |
| 99.99%       | four nines  | ~53 minutes       |
| 99.999%      | five nines  | ~5 minutes        |

Do it yourself: downtime = (1 − availability) × 525,600 min/year. So 99.99% → 0.0001 × 525,600 ≈ 53 min.

The point: nines sound close (99.9 vs 99.99) but each one is a 10× jump in cost and effort. Five nines is a
different engineering world from three — the last nines are the expensive ones.

</details>

### How much downtime are you allowed?
<details><summary>Show answer</summary>

Your **error budget** — the gap between 100% and your [SLO](2.1_Nonfunctional_Requirements.md#what-states-expected-performance).
If the SLO is 99.9% availability, the budget is the leftover **0.1%** — the downtime you're *allowed* to spend.

Concrete: 99.9% over a year ≈ 8.8 hours of permitted downtime. That's the budget for the year.

What it's for — it turns reliability into a spending decision, and settles the fight between shipping and stability:

- **budget left** → you can ship risky things (new features, big changes); there's room to absorb a mistake.
- **budget spent** → freeze features, stop and stabilize until you're back under SLO.

The point: 100% is never the target. A budget of zero means you can never take a risk, so you can never ship —
the leftover downtime is what *buys* the freedom to change the system.

</details>

### Rarely breaks, users still unhappy — why?
<details><summary>Show answer</summary>

**Verdict:** the system breaks seldom but stays broken a long time — high [MTTR](#how-fast-does-it-recover),
not low [MTBF](#how-often-does-it-break). You've been optimizing the wrong axis.

**Reasoning:** users feel *total downtime*, not failure count. Two rare outages of four hours each hurt far more
than a hundred blips of two seconds. A great "how often it breaks" number hides a terrible "how long it stays
down" number — and [availability](#how-available-is-the-system-overall) depends on both.

**Trade-off:** cutting MTTR (faster detection, automatic failover, quick rollback) buys more availability per
dollar than pushing MTBF higher once failures are already rare. The opposite call wins only when each failure is
catastrophic or unrecoverable — data loss, corruption — where preventing the failure at all beats recovering fast.

</details>

### Perfect uptime — what to consider?
<details><summary>Show answer</summary>

Teams often ask for 100% availability. It's the wrong target — not because it's hard, but because it's
self-defeating.

- **The cost curve is exponential.** Each extra nine costs ~10× more effort (three nines → five nines is a
  different engineering world). The last fraction of a percent is where the money disappears.
- **Zero budget means zero change.** 100% availability leaves an [error budget](#how-much-downtime-are-you-allowed)
  of nothing — every risky deploy could break the SLO, so you can never ship. A system you can't change is dead.
- **You don't control everything.** Upstream providers, networks, and hardware have their own failure rates. Your
  100% sits on top of things that aren't 100%.

So the real target is *enough* availability for the users' needs, deliberately below 100% — the leftover downtime
is what buys the freedom to keep changing the system.

</details>

### Redundant servers, all died together — how?
<details><summary>Show answer</summary>

**Verdict:** a correlated fault — almost always a shared **software** cause, not hardware. See
[hardware vs software faults](#hardware-faults-vs-software-faults).

**Reasoning:** redundancy assumes failures are *independent* — one disk dying says nothing about the next. Software
breaks that assumption: every node runs the same code, so one bug (or a bad input, or a config push) hits all
copies at once. Identical copies fail identically. Adding more of them just adds more things that break together.

**Trade-off:** hardware-style redundancy (more nodes, more disks) buys nothing against correlated faults — you need
different defenses: staged rollouts, canaries, config validation, killing bad inputs before they spread. The cost
is slower, more cautious deploys; the opposite call (push everywhere at once) only wins when speed matters more than
blast radius, which for shared code it rarely does.

</details>

### Availability from an outage log?
<details><summary><strong>Show details</strong></summary>

<details><summary>Show scenario</summary>

Over a **30-day** month the service had **3 outages** lasting **20, 40, and 30 minutes**.

Compute its availability.

</details>

<details><summary>Show answer</summary>

Work out each piece from the log:

- Total time in window: 30 × 24 × 60 = **43,200 min**
- Total downtime: 20 + 40 + 30 = **90 min** → up-time = 43,200 − 90 = **43,110 min**
- **MTTR** = downtime ÷ number of outages = 90 ÷ 3 = **30 min**
- **MTBF** = up-time ÷ number of outages = 43,110 ÷ 3 = **14,370 min**

Plug in:

    Availability = MTBF / (MTBF + MTTR) = 14,370 / (14,370 + 30) = 0.99792 ≈ 99.79%

**Shortcut check:** the ÷3 cancels top and bottom, so availability = up-time / total = 43,110 / 43,200 = same 99.79%.
Roughly three nines.

**The lesson:** cut MTTR from 30 min to 10 min and availability climbs to ~99.93% — same failure count, higher
availability. Recovery speed moves the number, not just how often it breaks.

</details>

</details>

### Required MTBF for a target?
<details><summary><strong>Show details</strong></summary>

<details><summary>Show scenario</summary>

You must hit **99.9%** availability. Your **MTTR is 1 hour** — that's how long recovery takes and you can't cut it.

How long must the system run between failures (**MTBF**) to reach the target?

</details>

<details><summary>Show answer</summary>

Rearrange the availability formula to solve for MTBF:

    Availability = MTBF / (MTBF + MTTR)

    →  MTBF = Availability × MTTR / (1 − Availability)

Plug in (Availability = 0.999, MTTR = 1 h):

    MTBF = 0.999 × 1 / (1 − 0.999) = 0.999 / 0.001 = 999 hours ≈ 41.6 days

**Read it back:** with a 1-hour recovery, the system may fail **at most once every ~42 days** to stay at three nines.
Want four nines with the same 1-hour MTTR? Then (1 − 0.9999) = 0.0001 → MTBF ≈ 9,990 h ≈ 416 days — 10× rarer.

**The lesson:** the two levers trade against each other. If you can't make MTTR smaller, the only way to a higher
target is failing far less often — and each extra nine demands a 10× longer gap between failures.

</details>

</details>

### Survive a whole datacenter going down?
<details><summary>Show answer</summary>

**Verdict:** don't defend a single machine — spread across machines and tolerate node loss **in software**. See
[cloud vs single node](#tolerating-hardware-faults--cloud-vs-single-node).

**Reasoning:** hardware redundancy inside one box (RAID, dual power) assumes *independent* faults, but a datacenter
outage takes the whole box — every internal spare with it. The only defense at that scale is copies in physically
separate locations. **Availability zones** tell you which resources are co-located, so you place redundancy in
*different* zones and the service survives losing a whole one.

**Trade-off:** cross-zone/region redundancy costs money (duplicated capacity) and latency (writes coordinate across
zones). The cheaper single-zone setup only wins when the workload can tolerate downtime during a zone loss.

</details>

### Walk me through incident response?
<details><summary>Show answer</summary>

**Verdict:** keep two questions in separate rooms — a **blameless** investigation, and performance management on its
own track. See [how to respond to an incident](#how-should-you-respond-to-an-incident).

**Reasoning:** blame at the moment of failure buys silence — people hide details to avoid punishment, and the
learning dies with them. A blameless postmortem gets full information, which usually points at the
**sociotechnical system** (priorities, incentives, missing funding) rather than one careless person. Whether
someone is skilled is a real question — just not one you ask *during* the postmortem.

**Trade-off:** blameless is sometimes misread as "competence never matters." It doesn't mean that — it means you
separate *learning from this incident* from *evaluating a person*, and run them at different times.

</details>

### Available but unreliable — how?
<details><summary>Show answer</summary>

The two words measure different things, so a system can score well on one and badly on the other:

- **Availability** — is it *up and answering*? Measured as up-time / total time.
- **Reliability** — is it *doing the right thing correctly*? Serving correct results without dropping or corrupting
  work.

**Available but unreliable:** the service responds fast to every request (100% up) but returns **wrong data**, loses
writes, or silently corrupts records. Every health check passes — it's "up" — yet it isn't doing its job. High
availability, broken reliability.

**The other way too:** a system can be *reliable but not available* — when it's up it's always correct, but it's
often down. Correct when present, just not present enough.

**The point:** "is it up?" and "is it correct?" are separate questions. Availability alone can hide a service that's
answering confidently with garbage.

</details>
